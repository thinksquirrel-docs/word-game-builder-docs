Understanding SPH

What is SPH?

SPH stands for Smoothed-particle hydrodynamics and is a way to calculate fluid flow and dynamics, among other things. It was initially developed in 1977 - recent advances in technology and methods have enabled SPH to run in real-time. Fluids are broken down into particles, each with a range of influence on other particles.

SPH in real-time is used for both liquid and gas simulation, and works well with existing physics engines. Fluvio's solver is driven by real-time SPH simulation.

Smoothing distances

The interaction range of each particle is known as its smoothing distance, or smoothing length. Particles move from areas of higher density to areas of lower density. As they move and apply forces on each other, their properties change. This produces a close approximation of the complex equations that go into fluid flow.

Smoothing distance is one of the most important propeties of SPH, as it affects the resolution of a fluid. With a smaller smoothing distance, more particles are needed to fill a volume compared to a larger smoothing distance. Higher resolution fluids have an increased performance cost.

Neighbor search and the broad phase

In real-time SPH, the most performance-critical operation is known as the neighbor search. This is the phase in which particles find their neighboring particles in order to do other calculations. This is extremely optimized in Fluvio, using a spatial partitioning grid. This grid keeps track of the positions of particles, in order to make neighbor searches faster. The size of this grid can be set in the inspector.

Boundary conditions

Boundary conditions are collisions between particles and solid masses. In Fluvio, these are handled through Unity by either Shuriken's collision module, or the Fluid Rigidbody Particles component.
